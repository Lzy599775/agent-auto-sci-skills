# Section Patterns

## Title

A strong title tells the reader what is measured, what spatial or social problem is addressed, and what lens, method, or scale makes the study distinctive.

Use one of these skeletons:

- `[Object] accessibility and [equity problem] in [place]: [method/lens]`
- `From [metric] to [broader concept]: [object] equity under [context]`
- `Coupling [resource exposure] and [sports opportunity] to identify [fairness problem]`
- `Who benefits from [resource]? [group/scale] evidence from [place]`
- `Revealing [mechanism] behind [spatial inequality] using [model/data]`

## Abstract

Use a six-sentence abstract:

1. Pressure: why sport/green/heat equity matters now.
2. Gap: what existing accessibility or exposure studies miss.
3. Purpose: what this study evaluates or explains.
4. Data and methods: multi-source data, model, equity metrics, mechanism model.
5. Findings: two to three specific quantified results.
6. Implication: planning, theory, or indicator contribution.

## Introduction

Use five paragraphs:

1. public problem;
2. object value;
3. existing method landscape;
4. precise gap;
5. objectives and contribution.

Gap template:

> However, three limitations remain. First, sports facility supply is often represented by facility counts or POI locations, which obscures service capacity, facility diversity, openness, and environmental suitability. Second, demand is commonly measured from residential population alone, with limited attention to age-specific needs, high-density communities, and daily mobility. Third, many studies describe accessibility patterns but do not explain how built environment, socioeconomic conditions, green exposure, and heat exposure jointly generate opportunity gaps.

## Study Area

Include location, population/density, sport/green/heat problem, policy relevance, spatial heterogeneity, data feasibility, and generalizability.

Do not only report population and area. State why the city is a useful test bed for the research question.

## Data

Data writing is a reproducibility contract. Each data source must specify source, year, spatial unit, preprocessing, derived variable, bias, and role in the model.

Recommended columns:

| Data theme | Source | Year | Resolution/unit | Processing | Derived variable | Role | Limitations |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|

## Methods

Methods should move from concept to formula:

1. conceptual framework;
2. analysis unit;
3. supply capacity;
4. demand population;
5. travel impedance;
6. accessibility/exposure metric;
7. equity metric;
8. mechanism model;
9. validation and sensitivity.

## Results

Each Results subsection should begin with an interpretive claim, then cite the figure/table and key numbers.

Good openings:

- `Sports facility supply is spatially concentrated, but high supply does not always translate into high accessibility.`
- `Older adults face a sharper access deficit in high-density peripheral communities.`
- `Green exposure moderates the relationship between facility access and active-health opportunity.`

## Discussion

Use four blocks: main interpretation, literature dialogue, planning translation, and boundary.

Planning translation must specify where, for whom, and by which intervention.

## Conclusion

Use three to four short paragraphs: framework, findings, mechanisms/planning, contribution/boundary. Do not introduce new references, new numbers, or new policy claims.
