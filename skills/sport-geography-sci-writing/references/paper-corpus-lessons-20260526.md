# Paper Corpus Lessons 2026-05-26

## Corpus

The local scan covered 56 PDF entries and 55 unique files from private literature folders. The source folders are intentionally not included in this public package.

The corpus includes sport facilities, urban parks, green exposure, heat exposure, 15-minute city accessibility, mobile-phone-based park use, environmental justice, green inequality, LCZ cooling, and physical activity/mental health links.

## Repeated Article Logic

Most strong papers use a seven-step logic:

1. A broad urban problem is made concrete: health, fairness, heat risk, mobility, urbanization, or public-service access.
2. A spatial resource is positioned as a mechanism of opportunity: parks, sports facilities, green exposure, cooling, or activity space.
3. Existing measures are shown to miss one part of reality: entrance, capacity, quality, group demand, mobile population, route impedance, background environment, or actual use.
4. A refined framework is introduced: improved 2SFCA/G2SFCA, real-time navigation, mobile phone data, remote sensing object detection, random forest, SHAP, Gini/Theil, spatial regression, or optimization.
5. Results move from pattern to inequality to drivers.
6. Discussion translates drivers into planning action.
7. Limitations state the boundary between accessibility, exposure, use, and health benefit.

## High-Value Moves for Sport Geography

- Reframe sports facilities as **active-health infrastructure**, not just POI supply.
- Reframe sports parks as **compound green, thermal, and activity opportunity spaces**.
- Treat facility accessibility as a necessary but incomplete condition for exercise opportunity.
- Pair horizontal equity and vertical equity.
- Separate four layers: physical supply, travel opportunity, environmental suitability, and social demand.

## Title Patterns Learned

Strong titles normally contain three of four elements: object, problem, method/lens, and place/scale.

Recommended sport geography title formulas:

- `From accessibility to exercise opportunity: spatial and social equity of sports facilities in [city]`
- `Green and thermal constraints on sports facility equity: evidence from [study area]`
- `Measuring active-health infrastructure equity through multi-source accessibility and exposure data`
- `Who gains exercise opportunities in the 15-minute city? A multi-group assessment of sports facility accessibility`
- `Coupling sports facility access with green and heat exposure to identify active-health opportunity gaps`

## Abstract Patterns Learned

The best abstracts use six sentences:

1. Why the issue matters.
2. What current research misses.
3. What the study does and where.
4. What data and model are used.
5. What the two or three core findings are.
6. What the planning or theoretical implication is.

## Introduction Patterns Learned

Effective introductions move from broad pressure to specific gap:

1. Urbanization, aging, public health, heat risk, or sustainable city pressure.
2. Parks, green spaces, and sports facilities as daily health-supporting infrastructure.
3. Existing measurement traditions: 2SFCA variants, network analysis, Gini/Theil, Lorenz, spatial regression, RF/XGBoost/SHAP.
4. Specific gap: supply simplified, demand static, quality/environment omitted, accessibility not linked to equity, mechanisms underdeveloped.
5. Study objectives as three research questions.

## Figure Patterns Learned

Typical main figure sequence:

1. Study area and resource distribution.
2. Research framework or workflow.
3. Supply capacity or environmental exposure.
4. Accessibility/exposure maps.
5. Equity, Lorenz/Gini, or mismatch maps.
6. Mechanism model: regression, SHAP, or interaction.
7. Scenario or optimization map.

## Table Patterns Learned

Useful tables:

- Table 1: data sources, years, resolution, processing, role.
- Table 2: variables and descriptive statistics.
- Table 3: accessibility/equity by region or group.
- Table 4: model results or variable importance.
- Table 5: robustness, sensitivity, or scenario comparison.
