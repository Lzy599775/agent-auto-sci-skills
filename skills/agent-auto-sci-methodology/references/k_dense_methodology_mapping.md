# K-Dense Methodology Mapping

Use this reference when adapting K-Dense hypothesis generation, critical thinking, scholar evaluation, and peer-review logic to geography + sport science.

## 1. Included Skill Families

| K-Dense skill family | Local use | Domain adaptation |
|---|---|---|
| hypothesis generation | turn broad interests into testable questions | exposure-accessibility-health and spatial-equity mechanisms |
| scientific critical thinking | critique claims, assumptions, and evidence | causal boundaries, MAUP, selection bias, ecological inference |
| scholar evaluation | assess article quality and field value | target-journal positioning and contribution audit |
| peer review | find weaknesses before reviewers do | methods, evidence, figures, policy agenda |
| scientific brainstorming | generate route options | conservative idea matrix rather than speculative topics |

## 2. Research Question Audit

A strong question states:

- population or place;
- exposure/accessibility construct;
- comparison or mechanism;
- spatial and temporal scale;
- outcome or planning implication;
- method route;
- contribution beyond mapping.

Weak question:

`What is the distribution of parks?`

Stronger question:

`How do sport-facility accessibility and green/heat exposure jointly shape potential active-living opportunities for vulnerable communities, and which planning levers can reduce spatial inequity?`

## 3. Hypothesis Matrix

| field | question |
|---|---|
| `hypothesis` | what relationship is expected? |
| `mechanism` | why should it occur? |
| `alternative` | what else could explain the pattern? |
| `observable` | what data would support it? |
| `threat` | what bias/confounder could mislead us? |
| `policy_relevance` | what decision could change if true? |

## 4. Critical-Thinking Checklist

Ask:

- Are constructs defined before metrics?
- Is the unit of analysis defensible?
- Does the method support the claim strength?
- Are spatial scale and boundary effects considered?
- Are vulnerable groups measured or merely named?
- Is policy language linked to evidence?
- Are null, mixed, or contradictory findings preserved?

## 5. Evidence Grading

| Evidence type | Typical strength | Caution |
|---|---|---|
| bibliometric co-occurrence | low for substantive claims | maps attention, not evidence |
| cross-sectional spatial association | low to moderate | confounding and ecological inference |
| longitudinal panel | moderate | still needs causal identification |
| quasi-experimental design | moderate to high | depends on assumptions |
| survey/use data with exposure | moderate | selection and self-report bias |
| triangulated review evidence | moderate to high | depends on coding and heterogeneity |

## 6. Novelty Audit

Novelty is stronger when it changes:

- concept boundary;
- measurement method;
- evidence synthesis framework;
- spatial scale or population focus;
- policy translation;
- reproducible workflow.

Novelty is weak when it is only:

- a new keyword combination;
- another bibliometric map with generic clusters;
- a single local case without transferable argument;
- ML added without research need.

## 7. Method-Choice Guardrail

Choose the simplest method that answers the question. Escalate only when necessary:

descriptive map -> inequality metric -> spatial statistics -> regression/quasi-experiment -> ML/XAI -> mixed-method synthesis.
