## 2. Shared Pattern in Strong Review Papers

High-level review and bibliometric papers usually have this logic:

```text
urban pressure -> knowledge fragmentation -> transparent evidence retrieval -> macro field mapping -> manual interpretation or coding -> mechanism/framework -> policy or research agenda
```

For the user's topic, translate this into:

```text
urban health and active living pressure -> unequal spatial opportunity -> fragmented measurement of sport/green/heat exposure -> bibliometric and evidence mapping -> critical coding of access-use-health-equity pathways -> equity-centered sport-green infrastructure agenda
```

## 3. Journal-Specific Positioning

### 3.1 Sustainable Cities and Society

SCS prefers review papers that connect evidence to urban sustainability transitions, resilience, governance, climate, smart/green infrastructure, or health-supportive planning.

For a sport geography review, the strongest SCS route is:

```text
active green infrastructure for sustainable and healthy cities:
bibliometric mapping + critical coding of sport/green exposure, heat mitigation, physical activity, and equity
```

Must emphasize:
- sustainable urban development and health-supportive environments;
- integration of green, cooling, and active living infrastructure;
- policy translation from diagnosis to intervention;
- cross-scale governance from park/facility to neighborhood and city.

Avoid:
- only describing publication counts;
- treating sports parks as a narrow facility category without linking to sustainability and health;
- recommendations that only say "increase green space" or "build more facilities".

### 3.2 Cities

Cities values conceptual clarity, urban theory, governance relevance, spatial inequality, everyday urban experience, and policy implications.

For a sport geography review, the strongest Cities route is:

```text
who gets active urban space:
a critical review of sport facilities, parks, green exposure, and spatial justice in cities
```

Must emphasize:
- urban inequality and spatial justice;
- actual use, perception, and lived experience;
- planning and governance mechanisms;
- why access metrics alone are insufficient.

Avoid:
- a health-only framing with weak urban theory;
- technical bibliometric figures without conceptual interpretation;
- treating city context as a background rather than the analytical object.

### 3.3 Nature Cities

Nature Cities requires a broader and more original contribution than a standard bibliometric review. A manuscript must be global or conceptually field-shaping, with transparent data and a strong urban policy message.

Possible route:

```text
global evidence gaps in equitable active green infrastructure:
a systematic stocktake of sport, park, green and heat exposure research
```

Requirements:
- large, transparent corpus;
- reproducible search and data availability;
- global or cross-regional relevance;
- a framework that changes how the field is understood;
- clear public-facing and policy-facing contribution.

Risk:
- a China-only or narrow sports-park-only bibliometric paper is unlikely to fit unless it produces a transferable theory or method.

### 3.4 Landscape and Urban Planning

LUP favors spatial planning, landscape configuration, exposure measurement, scale effects, design translation, and evidence maps.

Strong route:

```text
from green-space quantity to active and equitable exposure:
a systematic map of spatial metrics linking parks, sport facilities, heat mitigation and health
```

Must emphasize:
- spatial metrics and scale;
- landscape configuration and facility quality;
- planning implications;
- why findings diverge across methods and contexts.

### 3.5 Urban Forestry & Urban Greening

UFUG is suitable if the manuscript foregrounds urban green space, urban forest, quality, ecosystem services, public health, and social inequity.

Strong route:

```text
green-space quality, sport affordance and environmental justice:
a scoping review and bibliometric synthesis
```

Must emphasize:
- quantity versus quality;
- biodiversity/ecosystem service plus social use;
- vulnerable groups and green inequity;
- governance of urban green infrastructure.

### 3.6 Environment International / Environmental Research / Health & Place

These journals are stronger fits when the manuscript foregrounds exposure, intervention, health outcome, epidemiological evidence, or environmental justice.

Strong route:

```text
urban green and sport-space exposure pathways to physical activity and health:
a systematic evidence synthesis with equity coding
```

Must emphasize:
- exposure-response or pathway logic;
- bias and study-quality appraisal;
- confounding and causal caution;
- health equity and vulnerable groups;
- unintended effects, such as green gentrification or heat-risk displacement.

## 4. Recommended Route for the Current Manuscript

The safest high-level route for Cities/SCS is not "sports park exposure bibliometric analysis" alone.

Recommended title direction:

```text
Equitable active green infrastructure in cities:
a bibliometric and critical review of sport facilities, parks, green exposure and urban health
```

Core argument:

```text
Existing research separately examines parks, green space, sport facilities, accessibility, heat mitigation and physical activity. However, these literatures remain fragmented across object definitions, exposure metrics, spatial scales, population groups and justice dimensions. A hybrid bibliometric and critical coding review can reveal how the field has evolved, where evidence is concentrated, and how planning can move from equal provision toward equitable active green infrastructure.
```

Four research questions:

1. How has knowledge on sport/park/green exposure, physical activity, health and equity evolved across disciplines and regions?
2. What concepts, methods and spatial metrics dominate the field, and how have hotspots shifted over time?
3. How do existing studies connect supply, exposure, accessibility, actual use, health outcomes and equity?
4. What planning and governance agenda can translate evidence into equitable active green infrastructure?

Contribution statement:

```text
This review contributes by integrating bibliometric mapping with a critical coding framework that distinguishes spatial supply, exposure, accessibility, actual use, health pathways and justice dimensions. It moves beyond descriptive scientometric mapping by identifying measurement gaps, population blind spots and planning levers for equitable active green infrastructure.
```

## 5. Required Manual Coding Fields for a Cities/SCS-Level Paper

Do not rely on bibliometric maps alone. Add a manual coding table with at least these fields:

| Field | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Object type | Separates sports parks, sport facilities, parks, greenways, urban forests, pocket parks, and blue-green infrastructure. |
| Exposure/access metric | Prevents confusion between availability, proximity, quality, visibility, heat exposure, and perceived access. |
| Actual use measure | Distinguishes potential opportunity from observed behavior. |
| Health/activity outcome | Shows whether the study measures physical activity, mental health, heat stress, wellbeing, or only environmental exposure. |
| Spatial scale | Captures city, neighborhood, buffer, network, activity space, and facility-level differences. |
| Population group | Identifies children, older adults, low-income groups, women, disabled people, migrants, racial/ethnic minorities, and other groups. |
| Justice dimension | Separates distributional, procedural, recognitional, and capability justice. |
| Data source | Identifies GIS, remote sensing, street view, survey, GPS, SOPARC, mobile phone, social media, administrative data, and mixed methods. |
| Method family | Helps explain divergent conclusions. |
| Policy lever | Converts evidence into planning action. |
| Evidence strength | Prevents overclaiming. |

## 6. Figures That Fit High-Level Review Papers

For Cities/SCS:

1. PRISMA flow diagram.
2. Conceptual boundary diagram: supply -> exposure/access -> use -> health -> equity -> policy.
3. Bibliometric field evolution: annual output + keyword thematic evolution.
4. Knowledge network: co-word or co-citation clusters, cleaned with a thesaurus.
5. Critical evidence map: object type by outcome by justice dimension.
6. Method-scale matrix: exposure metric by spatial scale and data source.
7. Policy agenda matrix: diagnosis -> planning lever -> governance actor -> monitoring indicator.

For Nature Cities:

1. Global corpus map and coverage bias.
2. Field-wide thematic map.
3. Reproducible evidence gap atlas.
4. Conceptual framework with clear global policy relevance.
5. Source data and transparent supplementary tables.

## 7. Common Desk-Rejection Risks

| Risk | Why it matters | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Bibliometric analysis is the only contribution | High-level journals see this as descriptive | Add manual coding, theory, and policy agenda |
| Topic too narrow as "sports park exposure" | Corpus may be small and conceptually isolated | Reframe as active green infrastructure or sport-green urban space |
| Access equals use | This is a common conceptual error | Code actual use and perceived barriers separately |
| Exposure equals health benefit | Health effects need evidence and causal caution | Use pathway language and evidence-strength grading |
| Equity is only spatial equality | Equity includes need, quality, participation, recognition, capability | Use justice dimensions |
| Policy recommendations are generic | Journals expect actionable governance implications | Build diagnosis-to-action matrix |
| Search strategy is not reproducible | Systematic review credibility collapses | Report exact query, date, filters, and PRISMA flow |
| Software screenshots dominate | VOSviewer/CiteSpace outputs cannot replace interpretation | Make every figure answer an RQ |
