# Sport Geography Communication Playbook

Use this reference for geography + sport science manuscripts, reviews, rebuttals, slides, and policy communication.

## 1. Journal Route Logic

| Route | Best positioning | Risk to avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Cities | urban governance, planning relevance, spatial inequality, policy mechanism | purely bibliometric description without urban argument |
| Sustainable Cities and Society | sustainability, city systems, environment-health-planning link | generic "sustainable development" wording |
| Urban Forestry & Urban Greening | green-space function, ecosystem services, active living, tree/park quality | treating all green space as equal |
| Environment International | environmental exposure and health relevance | weak exposure assessment or no health pathway |
| Nature Cities | strong urban theory, global relevance, concise evidence | local case without conceptual contribution |
| Nature Human Behaviour / health-adjacent venues | behavior, health mechanism, population evidence | planning-only claims without behavioral or health data |

## 2. Strong Narrative Chain

Use the local sport-geography chain:

`real-world urban health pressure -> spatial inequity -> measurement gap -> better evidence -> mechanism explanation -> governance or planning agenda`

This chain is stronger than:

`topic is hot -> bibliometric map -> keyword clusters -> generic recommendations`.

## 3. Article-Type Routes

| Article type | Core contribution |
|---|---|
| systematic review | transparent evidence synthesis and evidence strength |
| scoping review | map concepts, methods, measures, and gaps in an emerging field |
| bibliometric + critical review | combine knowledge structure with deep manual coding and policy agenda |
| empirical paper | produce defensible spatial evidence and implications |
| methods paper | improve measurement of exposure/accessibility/equity |
| perspective/policy agenda | synthesize evidence into a framework and governance priorities |

## 4. Review Manuscript Structure

Recommended high-standard structure:

1. Introduction: urban-health and spatial-equity problem, not only literature volume.
2. Methods: database, query, screening, coding, bibliometric tools, limitations.
3. Knowledge structure: evolution, themes, intellectual base.
4. Evidence synthesis: metrics, populations, spatial scales, outcomes, methods.
5. Critical framework: what the field misses and why it matters.
6. Policy agenda: actionable planning/data/governance recommendations.
7. Conclusion: narrow, high-value claims.

## 5. Empirical Manuscript Structure

1. Problem and contribution.
2. Study area and data.
3. Exposure/accessibility/equity measurement.
4. Robustness and sensitivity.
5. Results as claims supported by maps/tables.
6. Mechanism and planning implications.
7. Limits: potential access, data quality, spatial scale, causality.

## 6. Language Boundaries

Prefer:

- "potential accessibility" when no behavior data exist;
- "exposure proxy" when using NDVI/LST/LCZ;
- "spatial inequity" when distribution differs by need or vulnerability;
- "planning implications" when evidence supports spatial intervention.

Avoid:

- "causes" without causal design;
- "health benefits" without health or behavior evidence;
- "fair" or "just" without a normative/evidence basis;
- "smart city" or "sustainability" as generic labels.

## 7. Policy Agenda Formula

A strong policy agenda links:

`evidence gap -> affected population -> spatial mechanism -> intervention lever -> data requirement -> implementation risk`

Example:

`High heat exposure around sport facilities for older adults -> shade and cooling retrofit -> combine facility audit with LST and use data -> avoid building new facilities where existing quality and thermal safety are the binding constraints.`
